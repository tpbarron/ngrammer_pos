<p>
<s>among the caribbean takeaways and moneytransfer haunts on a brixton high street stands ms cupcake a brightly coloured shop selling “the naughtiest vegan cakes in town”</s>
<s>what began as a home experiment in 2010 is now a thriving business—a feat of entrepreneurship when money for fripperies is tight</s>
<s>“it’s an affordable treat” explains melissa morgan the real ms cupcake</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>british high streets are depressed particularly outside london</s>
<s>vacancy rates which stood at 31 in 2008 have stabilised at around 15 with more empty shops in the north and midlands</s>
<s>in the first half of this year an average of 20 chain stores a day closed in the biggest 500 town centres according to the local data company a retail information source and pricewaterhousecoopers an accountancy firm</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>cyclical decline in spending is only one reason structural shifts to outoftown shopping centres and the internet also figure</s>
<s>as jobs have migrated to enterprise zones and office parks outside city centres fewer shoppers regularly visit the high street</s>
<s>gone or dwindling are chain stores selling furniture computer games jewellery and toys</s>
<s>the collapse of jjb sports a sports retailer in october is the latest in a string of failures that has claimed clinton cards game group a videogame retailer and peacocks a bargain fashion chain</s>
<s>large companies are concentrating on fewer shops in prime locations</s>
<s>retailers doing well such as next which sells fashion and homeware and john lewis an upmarket department store have embraced online shopping with easy instore returns</s>
<s>but not all stores are suffering equally</s>
<s>independently owned ones which account for twothirds of britain’s high streets have fared better than chains growing modestly in number in the past two years—though that rate is slowing</s>
<s>the number of independent bakeries on high streets rose by 17 from august 2011 to july 2012 according to simply business an insurance provider</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>besides quirky independent outlets the businesses faring best often provide a service—pawnbrokers and bookmakers in poor neighbourhoods boutiques in wealthier places and beauty salons reflecting a recent british fetish for nailcare everywhere</s>
<s>they tend to have low startup costs and complement the big chains rather than competing with them</s>
<s>“the places that provide an experience or are conveniently located will survive the rise of the internet” says alex morton at policy exchange a thinktank which is researching a report on high streets</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>the government has now pledged to make it easier to open popup shops and has committed over £80m $130m in loans for young entrepreneurs</s>
<s>retailers say local authorities should also make it easier for different businesses to fill vacant units</s>
<s>but rents parking and taxes remain hurdles</s>
<s>the british retail consortium complains that business rates set according to september inflation figures will result in a nationwide increase of £175m next year on top of hikes over the last two years</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>against these odds mary portas a journalist and shopping guru has proposed ideas to revive high streets which include less onerous parking rules and more local investment</s>
<s>two dozen towns—“portas pilots”—are receiving government funding and advice on how to revive flagging shopping centres</s>
<s>other towns are meant to emulate them</s>
<s>but interest in sweeping schemes has waned</s>
<s>more prosaically a new task force of landlords and bank officials is assessing what to do with indebted properties</s>
<s>many high streets are unlikely to regain their former glory</s>
<s>still an eye for a gap in the market pays off</s>
<s>where ms cupcake leads others can follow</s>
</p>
