<s>it is republic day in mumbai and an elderly nun addresses 1000 silent schoolgirls gathered in the playground of mary immaculate girls school</s>

<p>
<s>despite a rapid rise in attendance since 2000 72m schoolage children across the world are still not in school half of them in subsaharan africa and a quarter in south and west asia</s>
<s>the united nations reckons it would cost $16 billion a year to get the remaining stragglers into class by 2015—one of its big development goals</s>
<s>yet a free education is something that many parents will pay to avoid</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>in india for example between a quarter and a third of pupils attend private schools according to the oecd a parisbased thinktank and others have private tutors</s>
<s>in cities the proportion is more like 85 reckons geeta kingdon who conducts research in mumbai and elsewhere for the institute of education in london</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>a government decision in 2007 to make primary schooling compulsory and free boosted privateschool numbers</s>
<s>many parents became disenchanted with stateschool teachers who failed to show up or taught badly—by for example failing to correct errors</s>
<s>surveys by pratham a mumbaibased charity suggest that standards in state schools slipped as the system expanded whereas in the private sector they have held up</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>in china too lowfee private schools have emerged but less because the state schools are bad than because migrants lose the right to a free state education for their offspring</s>
<s>in beijing alone some 500000 migrant children cannot get into a state school</s>
<s>many are taught in unlicensed private schools which unlike their indian equivalents tend to be downatheel compared with state provision</s>

<p>
<s>in african countries such as ghana nigeria and uganda teaching is all too often a sinecure not a vocation</s>
<s>governments have built many new schools but cannot dismiss even the worst teachers</s>
<s>poor instruction by teachers who physically beat their pupils is rife</s>
<s>in private schools the parents are choosy customers</s>
<s>they care more about the quality of instruction than the snazziness of the premises</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>james tooley of the university of newcastle has pioneered the study of cheap private schools in poor countries</s>
<s>he has also set some up</s>
<s>his research published in 2009 in a book called “the beautiful tree” often surprised local officials who were unaware such schools existed</s>
<s>mr tooley describes classes in the front rooms of peoples houses often as an extension of basic child care</s>
<s>most are run for profit—though even these may offer free places for orphans and other needy children</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>but the private sector faces problems from bossy bureaucrats especially in india</s>
<s>it is illegal there to operate a school for profit so schools that charge fees must act as charities first and businesses second</s>
<s>the right to education act which came into effect in 2010 compels all independent schools to register with the government on pain of closure surveys suggest that only about half bother to do so</s>
<s>the same law also compels private schools to take a quarter of their students from poor families</s>
<s>many have resisted not least because the subsidies that were supposed to pay for the places have not been forthcoming</s>
<s>some state courts have ruled that privateschool teachers must have the same high pay as state ones and have mandated budgetbusting facilities such as large playgrounds and libraries</s>
</p>

<p>
<s>big aid organisations and charities have long been sceptical of the private schools arguing that they increase inequality and undermine state provision</s>
<s>tove wang of save the children a charity doubts if private schools however plentiful can ever cater for the very poorest</s>
<s>she points to research indicating that poor parents go private only when state schools are dire if the publicly financed ones improved she argues they would be more popular</s>

<p>
